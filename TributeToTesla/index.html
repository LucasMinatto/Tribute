Nikola Tesla

The genius who electrified the world

Here's a little about Nikola's life:

1856 - Born in Smiljan, Austrian Empire (modern-day Croatia) on July 10. His father was a priest, and his mother, though unschooled, was a talented inventor of household devices.

1870s - Studies engineering at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz and later at Charles-Ferdinand University in Prague. Leaves university before graduating.

1881 - Works in Budapest, Hungary, where he conceives the idea for the rotating magnetic field, a fundamental principle of his later inventions.

1882 - Moves to Paris to work for the Continental Edison Company, designing and improving electrical equipment.

1884 - Emigrates to the United States. Begins working with Thomas Edison in New York but resigns due to conflicting ideas and business disagreements.

1885 - Secures funding for the Tesla Electric Light Company but is forced out of the business. Left digging ditches to make ends meet, he vows to continue his research.

1887 - Develops the induction motor and begins promoting alternating current (AC), which becomes a cornerstone of modern electricity.

1888 - Partners with George Westinghouse to commercialize AC. This alliance sparks the “War of the Currents” with Edison, who advocates for direct current (DC).

1891 - Invents the Tesla coil, which becomes essential in wireless communication and experiments with high-voltage electricity. Becomes a U.S. citizen.

1893 - Plays a crucial role in lighting the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, showcasing the superiority of AC over DC.

1895 - Builds one of the world's first hydroelectric power plants at Niagara Falls, in collaboration with Westinghouse.

1898 - Demonstrates a radio-controlled boat, an early step toward wireless technology and remote control systems.

1900 - Begins work on the Wardenclyffe Tower, a massive project aimed at providing wireless global communication and free energy. The project is abandoned due to financial difficulties.

1917 - Receives the Edison Medal, the highest honor from the American Institute of Electrical Engineers, for his contributions to the development of electricity.

1920s–1930s - Lives in relative obscurity, though he continues to propose radical ideas, including plans for wireless energy transmission and advanced weaponry.

1943 - Dies alone on January 7 in a New York City hotel room at the age of 86. Later that year, the U.S. Supreme Court credits him posthumously for the invention of the radio.

Legacy

Tesla's work laid the foundation for countless technologies, including modern power systems, wireless communication, and robotics. Often underappreciated in his lifetime, he is now celebrated as one of history's greatest inventors, a visionary who dreamed far beyond the limits of his era.
